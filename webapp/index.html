Coral Reefs at the Breaking Point

Which reefs are most at risk of collapse, and which communities will lose food security and income?

Abstract. The 4th Global Coral Bleaching Event (2023-2025) has affected 84% of the world's reef area. Using the Global Coral Bleaching Database, NOAA Coral Reef Watch SST data, and socioeconomic dependency indices, we identify the 30 most at-risk reef systems and quantify the human communities that depend on them for food and income. Our analysis reveals that over 500 million people face food-security and livelihood impacts, with small island developing states disproportionately affected. Counterintuitively, reefs exposed to high historical temperature variance show greater resilience — a finding with direct policy implications for marine protected area prioritization.

Methodology

Combining ecological and socioeconomic data

We merged the Global Coral Bleaching Database (GCBD, 40,000+ survey records) with NOAA Coral Reef Watch sea surface temperature anomalies and WorldPop coastal population grids. Bleaching severity was modeled as a function of Degree Heating Weeks (DHW), historical SST variance, reef depth, and local stressors. Community dependency was estimated using FAO fisheries data and national tourism statistics.

Risk scores combine ecological vulnerability (bleaching probability, recovery rate) with socioeconomic exposure (dependent population, economic value, alternative livelihood availability).
