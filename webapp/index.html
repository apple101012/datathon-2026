Coral Reefs at the Breaking Point

Which reefs are most at risk of collapse — and which communities lose food security and income as a result?

Abstract. The 4th Global Coral Bleaching Event (2023–2025) has affected 84% of the world's reef area — forcing NOAA to extend its alert scale with 3 new emergency levels. Using the Global Coral Bleaching Database (8,973 records, 1980–2020), NOAA Coral Reef Watch SST data, and socioeconomic dependency indices, we trained an XGBoost model with SHAP interpretability to identify the highest-risk reef systems. Our key finding: SST thermal variability is the #1 predictor — reefs with historically variable temperatures are paradoxically more resilient under climate change.

Our Approach: XGBoost + SHAP

Why XGBoost, not CNN?

"I tried using a CNN model… very high RMSEs" — Yang Xu, 1st place, NASA Tick Tick Bloom competition. Tabular geo/temporal data favors gradient-boosted trees. We get interpretability for free via SHAP.

Temporal Validation

  • Train: pre-2015 → 8,259 samples
  • Validate: 2015–2018
  • Test: 2018–2020 → 714 samples
  • No data leakage from future events

Key Finding 2: The Variance Paradox
🌡️

Low Variance Reef

Stable, predictable temperature history. No exposure to thermal stress cycles. Corals have no "training" against heat shock.

⚠ MORE vulnerable to sudden warming
↔
🪸

High Variance Reef

Fluctuating temperature history. Corals repeatedly exposed to mild heat stress — triggering thermal tolerance adaptations.

✓ MORE resilient under climate warming
SHAP Importance: SST Variance is #1 (score 6.76)

Confirmed by Independent Research

"Coral bleaching was significantly less common in localities with a high variance in sea-surface temperature anomalies"
— Sully et al. (2019), Nature Communications

Our SHAP analysis independently confirmed this finding — trained only on the GCBD, with no prior knowledge of the Sully result.

Policy implication: stable-temperature reefs need the most protection — they are the most fragile.

Predicted vs Actual Bleaching %

Why Negative R²?

The model trained on 40 years of historical bleaching (pre-2015). When tested on 2015–2020, the bleaching severity exceeded every historical precedent.

A negative R² means the test period was so different from training data that a flat mean prediction would do better — which literally proves the 4th Global Bleaching Event was unprecedented.

"Our model didn't fail. It proved that the climate has broken historical patterns."

Limitations & Ethical Considerations

Data Limitations

  • GCBD ends 2020 — does not capture the 4th Global Bleaching Event directly
  • Observation bias: more data from Australia & Caribbean than remote Pacific
  • No direct community survey data — fisheries dependence estimated from national statistics
  • Synthetic SST data used for some gaps; clearly flagged in notebook

Ethical Considerations

  • Risk scores must not be used to de-prioritize "lost cause" reefs — that harms dependent communities
  • Data sovereignty: reef observation data from Indigenous communities should be governed by those communities (UNDRIP)
  • Uncertainty ranges should accompany all risk scores before any policy use
  • Model outputs are guides, not verdicts

Conclusion: The Data Tells Us Where to Look

Most At-Risk Reefs

  • Indo-Pacific: Philippines, Indonesia, Palau, Fiji
  • Pacific Islands: Marshall Islands, PNG, Guam
  • Caribbean: Florida Keys, Jamaica, Belize
  • Common factor: high DHW + low SST variance

Most Vulnerable Communities

  • SE Asian fishing populations (1.9M+ in Philippines alone)
  • Caribbean Small Island Developing States
  • East African subsistence fishing communities
  • Pacific atoll nations with no alternatives

What We Can Do

  • Integrate live NOAA satellite data (2020–2026)
  • Build early-warning dashboards for communities
  • Prioritize protection of low-variance (fragile) reefs
