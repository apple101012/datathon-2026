Our Approach: XGBoost + SHAP

Why XGBoost, not CNN?

"I tried using a CNN model… very high RMSEs" — Yang Xu, 1st place, NASA Tick Tick Bloom competition.

Tabular geo/temporal data favors gradient-boosted trees. SHAP gives us interpretability for free — critical for policy recommendations.

  • n_estimators=200, max_depth=6, learning_rate=0.05
  • subsample=0.8, colsample_bytree=0.8
  • 8 engineered features: DHW, SSTA_SD, ClimSST, Mean SST, Depth, Turbidity, Cyclone freq, Latitude

Temporal Validation Split

  • Train: pre-2015 → 8,259 samples (92% of data)
  • Validate: 2015–2018 → hyperparameter tuning
  • Test: 2018–2020 → 714 samples (held-out)
  • Prevents leakage from future bleaching events
  • Mirrors real-world deployment: train on history, predict future

Key Finding 2: The Variance Paradox
🌡️

Low Variance Reef

Stable, predictable temperature history. Corals have never experienced heat stress cycles — no thermal conditioning.

⚠ MORE vulnerable to sudden warming
↔
🪸

High Variance Reef

Fluctuating temperature history. Repeated mild heat exposure triggers thermal tolerance adaptations in coral symbiosis.

✓ MORE resilient under climate warming
SHAP Top 5 — SST Variance is #1

Independently Confirmed

"Coral bleaching was significantly less common in localities with a high variance in sea-surface temperature anomalies."

Sully et al. (2019), Nature Communications

Policy: stable-temperature reefs need the most protection — they are the most fragile under sudden warming.

Why Negative R²?

The model trained on 40 years of historical bleaching (pre-2015). When tested on 2015–2020, the severity exceeded every historical precedent.

A negative R² means a flat-mean prediction beats the model — which literally proves the 4th Global Bleaching Event was unprecedented.

"Our model didn't fail. It proved that the climate has broken historical patterns."

Limitations & Ethical Considerations

Data Limitations

  • GCBD ends 2020 — does not capture the 4th Bleaching Event directly
  • Observation bias toward well-studied reefs (Australia, Caribbean)
  • No direct community survey data — dependency estimated from national statistics
  • Synthetic SST fallback used for some gaps; clearly flagged in notebook

Ethical Considerations

  • Risk scores must NOT be used to de-prioritize "lost cause" reefs — that harms dependent communities
  • Data sovereignty: reef data from Indigenous communities should be governed by those communities (UNDRIP)
  • Uncertainty ranges should accompany all risk scores before any policy use
  • Model outputs are guides, not verdicts
