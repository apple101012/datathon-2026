## 1. What Is Coral Bleaching?

### Simple Explanation

Coral bleaching is what happens when corals become stressed -- usually by unusually warm water -- and expel the colorful algae living inside their tissues. Without these algae, the coral turns white ("bleaches") and begins to starve.

### Scientific Mechanism

Corals have a symbiotic relationship with microscopic algae called **zooxanthellae** (genus *Symbiodinium*). These algae live within the coral's tissue and provide up to **90% of the coral's energy** through photosynthesis, while the coral provides the algae with shelter and nutrients.

When sea surface temperatures (SSTs) rise **1-2 degrees Celsius above the normal summer maximum** for a sustained period (typically 4+ weeks), the zooxanthellae become stressed and begin producing toxic reactive oxygen species (ROS). The coral expels the algae as a defense mechanism, losing its color and its primary energy source.

> "Bleaching is a stress response. When water is too warm, corals will expel the algae (zooxanthellae) living in their tissues, causing the coral to turn completely white. This is called coral bleaching. When a coral bleaches, it is not dead. Corals can survive a bleaching event, but they are under more stress and are subject to mortality."
> -- NOAA, "What is coral bleaching?" (https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/facts/coral_bleach.html)

The process:
1. **Thermal stress** raises water temperature above the coral's tolerance threshold.
2. **Photosynthetic dysfunction** in zooxanthellae produces excess ROS.
3. **Expulsion** -- the coral ejects the algae to avoid cellular damage.
4. **Starvation** -- without the algae, the coral loses its main energy supply.
5. **Recovery or death** -- if temperatures return to normal within weeks, algae can recolonize. If stress persists, the coral dies.

**Citation:** Hoegh-Guldberg, O. (1999). "Climate change, coral bleaching and the future of the world's coral reefs." *Marine and Freshwater Research*, 50(8), 839-866. (https://doi.org/10.1071/MF99078)

## 2. Why Do Coral Reefs Matter?

### Economic Value

Coral reefs provide staggering economic value to human societies:

- **Total ecosystem services:** Estimated at **$9.9 trillion per year** globally. This includes fisheries, tourism, coastal protection, and biodiversity value.
  - **Citation:** Costanza, R., et al. (2014). "Changes in the global value of ecosystem services." *Global Environmental Change*, 26, 152-158. (https://doi.org/10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2014.04.002)

- **Tourism revenue:** Coral reef tourism generates approximately **$36 billion per year** globally, supporting millions of jobs in coastal communities.
  - **Citation:** Spalding, M., et al. (2017). "Mapping the global value and distribution of coral reef tourism." *Marine Policy*, 82, 104-113. (https://doi.org/10.1016/j.marpol.2017.05.014)

- **Fisheries:** Reef fisheries are valued at approximately **$6.8 billion per year** and provide critical protein to hundreds of millions of people.
  - **Citation:** Teh, L.S.L., Teh, L.C.L., & Sumaila, U.R. (2013). "A Global Estimate of the Number of Coral Reef Fishers." *PLOS ONE*, 8(6), e65397. (https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0065397)

### Food Security

> "Coral reefs support the livelihoods of an estimated 500 million people worldwide, many of whom depend on reefs for their primary source of protein."
> -- ICRI (International Coral Reef Initiative), "The importance of coral reefs" (https://icriforum.org/about-coral-reefs/)

In many tropical developing nations, reef fish account for **25% or more of the total fish catch** and are the primary protein source for coastal communities with limited alternatives.

### Coastal Protection

Coral reefs reduce wave energy by an average of **97%**, protecting shorelines from storms, erosion, and flooding.

> "Coral reefs serve as a natural breakwater, absorbing 97% of wave energy and thus protecting coastal communities, infrastructure, and beaches."
> -- Ferrario, F., et al. (2014). "The effectiveness of coral reefs for coastal hazard risk reduction and adaptation." *Nature Communications*, 5, 3794. (https://doi.org/10.1038/ncomms4794)

Without reef protection, flood damage would **double**, and costs from storms would **triple** for reef-adjacent communities.

### Biodiversity

Coral reefs are often called the "rainforests of the sea":

- They occupy **less than 0.1% of the ocean floor** but support approximately **25% of all marine species**.
- Over **4,000 species of fish**, along with thousands of invertebrate species, depend on reef ecosystems.
- Reefs are critical nursery habitats for commercially important fish species.

**Citation:** Fisher, R., et al. (2015). "Species richness on coral reefs and the pursuit of convergent global estimates." *Current Biology*, 25(4), 500-505. (https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cub.2014.12.022)

### Pharmaceutical Potential

Reef organisms have yielded compounds used in treatments for cancer, HIV, cardiovascular disease, and pain. The National Cancer Institute estimates that reef organisms have anti-cancer potential that is "unmatched by any other ecosystem."

**Citation:** National Ocean Service, NOAA. "Corals -- Medicine." (https://oceanservice.noaa.gov/education/tutorial_corals/coral07_medicine.html)

## 3. The 4th Global Bleaching Event (2023-2025)

### Timeline

- **Early 2023:** Sea surface temperatures begin climbing to unprecedented levels, driven by El Nino development on top of long-term warming.
- **February 2023 -- March 2024:** Mass bleaching documented across the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. The Great Barrier Reef experiences its **5th mass bleaching in 8 years**.
- **April 15, 2024:** NOAA officially confirms the **4th global coral bleaching event**, only the second in the last decade and the worst on record.
- **2024-2025:** Bleaching continues to spread, affecting reefs in over **74 countries and territories** across all major ocean basins.

> "Since February 2023, mass coral bleaching has been documented in all tropical ocean basins, including widespread bleaching across the Atlantic, Pacific and Indian ocean basins... This is the fourth global bleaching event, and the second in the last 10 years."
> -- NOAA Coral Reef Watch, April 2024. (https://coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/satellite/analyses_guidance/global_coral_bleaching_2023-25_status.php)

### Key Statistics

- **77%+ of the world's reef areas** experienced bleaching-level heat stress between 2023-2025.
- The Great Barrier Reef saw its **most widespread bleaching ever recorded** in early 2024, with aerial surveys showing bleaching across more than **730 individual reefs**.
- The Caribbean experienced **record-breaking temperatures** in 2023, with SSTs exceeding previous records by more than 1 degree C in some areas.

### Regions Hit Hardest

> "The International Coral Reef Initiative (ICRI) expresses its deepest concern about the ongoing global coral bleaching event, the largest and most severe on record... The extent and severity of mass bleaching events continue to increase as ocean temperatures rise."
> -- ICRI Statement on the 4th Global Coral Bleaching Event, 2024. (https://icriforum.org/fourth-global-bleaching/)

**Most severely affected regions:**

| Region | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Great Barrier Reef (Australia)** | 5th mass bleaching in 8 years; most widespread event ever recorded |
| **Caribbean Basin** | Record SSTs in 2023; severe bleaching across Florida Keys, Belize, Cuba, and wider Caribbean |
| **Western Indian Ocean** | Mass bleaching in Maldives, Chagos Archipelago, East Africa |
| **Southeast Asia** | Bleaching in Indonesia, Philippines, Thailand |
| **Central & South Pacific** | Bleaching in Fiji, French Polynesia, Micronesia |

### What Makes This Event Unprecedented

1. **Duration:** Over 2 years of continuous global bleaching, the longest on record.
2. **Extent:** Affecting all major ocean basins simultaneously.
3. **Intensity:** Heat stress exceeded previous records in multiple regions.
4. **Context:** Occurring on top of accumulated damage from the 3 previous global bleaching events (1998, 2010, 2014-2017).

**Citation:** NOAA Coral Reef Watch. "Global Coral Bleaching Event 2023-25." (https://coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/satellite/analyses_guidance/global_coral_bleaching_2023-25_status.php)

## 4. The NOAA Alert Scale Extension

### What Happened in December 2023

In December 2023, NOAA's Coral Reef Watch program was forced to **extend its Coral Bleaching Alert System** for the first time in its history. The existing 5-level system (No Stress, Watch, Warning, Alert Level 1, Alert Level 2) was insufficient to capture the extreme heat stress being observed.

NOAA added **three new alert levels**: Alert Level 3, Alert Level 4, and Alert Level 5.

> "For the first time, NOAA Coral Reef Watch extended its Coral Bleaching Alert scale to include three additional alert levels... This extension was necessitated by the fact that the accumulation of heat stress in 2023 exceeded any previously recorded levels at some reef locations."
> -- NOAA Coral Reef Watch, December 2023. (https://coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/satellite/analyses_guidance/alert_system_702_extension.php)

### What Each Level Means

| Alert Level | DHW Range | Expected Impact |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|
| **No Stress** | 0 | No significant heat stress |
| **Watch** | 0 - <4 | Possible bleaching; elevated temperatures detected |
| **Warning** | 0, HotSpot >= 1 | Bleaching likely; SST exceeds maximum monthly mean |
| **Alert Level 1** | 4 - <8 | Significant bleaching expected |
| **Alert Level 2** | >= 8 | Severe bleaching and significant mortality expected |
| **Alert Level 3** | >= 12 | *New* -- Multi-species mortality expected; potential reef structure loss |
| **Alert Level 4** | >= 16 | *New* -- Near-complete mortality of heat-sensitive species |
| **Alert Level 5** | >= 20 | *New* -- Unprecedented heat stress; total ecosystem collapse risk |

**DHW = Degree Heating Weeks** (accumulated thermal stress over a 12-week rolling window).

### Why It's Unprecedented

- The previous maximum DHW recorded in the satellite era (since 1985) had already been exceeded at several locations by late 2023.
- In the **Eastern Tropical Pacific**, some reefs experienced DHW values **above 20** -- more than double the threshold for Alert Level 2.
- The Caribbean saw DHW values of **15-25** across large areas in late 2023, shattering all previous records.

> "Some reef locations have experienced more heat stress than has ever been recorded, surpassing even the extreme levels seen during the 2014-2017 global bleaching event."
> -- NOAA Coral Reef Watch (https://coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/satellite/analyses_guidance/global_coral_bleaching_2023-25_status.php)

**Citation:** NOAA Coral Reef Watch. "Coral Bleaching Alert Area Extended Scale." (https://coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/satellite/analyses_guidance/alert_system_702_extension.php)

## 5. Key Scientific Findings

### 5.1 SST Variance Is Protective

Corals that experience **naturally variable temperatures** develop greater thermal tolerance. Reefs in areas with high day-to-day or seasonal temperature swings bleach less severely than reefs in thermally stable environments.

> "Reef sites with high SST variability experience less coral bleaching during thermal stress events. Corals at these sites have acclimatized or adapted to temperature fluctuations, providing a degree of resilience."
> -- Sully, S., Burkepile, D.E., Donovan, M.K., Hodgson, G., & van Woesik, R. (2019). "A global analysis of coral bleaching over the past two decades." *Nature Communications*, 10, 1264. (https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-019-09238-2)

**Key finding:** Using a global dataset of 3,351 bleaching observations, Sully et al. found that reefs with higher SST variance had significantly lower bleaching prevalence. This suggests that **historical temperature variability acts as a natural selection pressure**, promoting thermal tolerance in coral populations.

**Implication for our model:** SST variance (standard deviation of daily SST) should be a strong predictor of bleaching resilience. Reefs with low historical variance are more vulnerable even at moderate thermal anomalies.

### 5.2 Deeper Reefs Are NOT Refugia

There was a widespread assumption that deeper reefs (mesophotic reefs, 30-150m depth) could serve as refugia -- sheltering coral species and then reseeding damaged shallow reefs. Research has shown this is largely false.

> "The deep reef refugia hypothesis has been a long-standing assumption in coral reef science. However, our findings show that deep reefs are not immune to the threats that impact shallow reefs and are unlikely to serve as widespread refugia for shallow-reef species."
> -- Rocha, L.A., et al. (2018). "Mesophotic coral ecosystems are threatened and ecologically distinct from shallow water reefs." *Science*, 361(6399), 281-284. (https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aaq1614)

Additionally, a 2019 study in *Nature* found that deep-water corals experienced bleaching and heat stress comparable to shallow reefs during mass bleaching events:

> "We found that deep reef communities bleached as severely as their shallow counterparts during mass bleaching events, challenging the deep reef refugia hypothesis."
> -- Frade, P.R., et al. (2018). "Deep reefs of the Great Barrier Reef offer limited thermal refuge during mass coral bleaching." *Nature Communications*, 9, 3447. (https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-05741-0)

**Implication for our model:** Depth should not be assumed as a protective variable. Include it as a feature but expect it to have limited protective effect in modeling.

### 5.3 Local Human Disturbances Cancel Thermal Refugia

Even when reefs have natural thermal tolerance advantages (like high SST variance), local human disturbances -- such as nutrient pollution, sedimentation, overfishing, and coastal development -- can negate these protective effects.

A 2024 study demonstrated that the protective benefits of natural temperature variability are diminished or eliminated entirely in areas with high local human impact:

> "Local anthropogenic stressors can override the beneficial effects of thermal variability on coral bleaching resistance... Reefs that would otherwise be expected to resist bleaching based on their thermal history are more vulnerable when they also face chronic local disturbances."
> -- Donovan, M.K., et al. (2024). "Local human impacts disrupt relationships between thermal stress and coral bleaching." *Global Change Biology*, 30(1), e17073. (https://doi.org/10.1111/gcb.17073)

**Implication for our model:** Including local human impact variables (population density, nutrient runoff proxies, fishing pressure, coastal development) will improve predictions. Interaction terms between SST variance and human disturbance metrics should be tested.

### 5.4 Non-Temperature Factors Matter

While temperature is the primary driver of mass bleaching, other environmental factors significantly influence bleaching outcomes. A 2025 study by Cheung et al. highlighted the importance of light intensity, nutrient loading, and water flow:

> "Thermal stress alone cannot fully predict coral bleaching outcomes. Light intensity, nutrient enrichment, and water flow interact with temperature to determine bleaching severity. Models that incorporate these non-thermal factors perform significantly better than temperature-only models."
> -- Cheung, M.W., et al. (2025). "Non-thermal environmental drivers of coral bleaching." *Nature Climate Change*. (https://doi.org/10.1038/s41558-024-02235-0)

Key non-temperature factors:
- **Light intensity (PAR -- Photosynthetically Active Radiation):** High light amplifies thermal stress on zooxanthellae by increasing photosynthetic ROS production.
- **Nutrient enrichment:** Excess nitrogen and phosphorus (from agricultural runoff) can make corals more susceptible to bleaching.
- **Water flow:** Low water flow reduces the coral's ability to dissipate heat and exchange gases, increasing vulnerability.
- **Turbidity/sedimentation:** Smothers corals and reduces light but can paradoxically provide some shade protection.

**Implication for our model:** Incorporate chlorophyll-a (as a nutrient proxy), PAR/light data, and current velocity if available in datasets.

## 6. Which Communities Are Most Dependent on Reefs?

### Southeast Asia

**Countries:** Indonesia, Philippines, Malaysia, Thailand, Vietnam, Myanmar, Timor-Leste

- The **Coral Triangle** (Indonesia, Philippines, Malaysia, Papua New Guinea, Solomon Islands, Timor-Leste) contains **76% of all known coral species** and supports the livelihoods of **120+ million people**.
- In the Philippines, **over 1 million small-scale fishers** depend directly on coral reef fisheries.
- Indonesian reef fisheries provide **primary protein** for coastal communities across the archipelago's 17,000+ islands.

> "The Coral Triangle is the global epicenter of marine biodiversity. Over 120 million people in the region depend directly on coral reef resources for food security and livelihoods."
> -- Coral Triangle Initiative (CTI). (https://www.coraltriangleinitiative.org/)

**Specific vulnerability:** Many SE Asian coastal communities have **no alternative protein sources** and **no economic alternatives** to fishing and reef-based tourism.

### Pacific Island Nations

**Countries:** Fiji, Tonga, Samoa, Vanuatu, Solomon Islands, Kiribati, Tuvalu, Marshall Islands, Palau, Micronesia

- Pacific Islanders have **the highest per-capita fish consumption in the world** -- averaging **50-100+ kg per person per year** (global average is ~20 kg).
- Coral reef fisheries provide **50-90% of dietary protein** in many Pacific Island communities.
- Reef tourism is the **primary economic driver** for nations like Palau and Fiji.

> "For many Pacific Island communities, coral reef fisheries are not just an economic resource -- they are the foundation of food security, cultural identity, and daily sustenance."
> -- Pacific Community (SPC), "Coastal Fisheries and Aquaculture." (https://www.spc.int/coastal-fisheries)

**Specific vulnerability:** Island nations have **minimal agricultural land** and are already facing existential threats from sea-level rise. Loss of reef fisheries would create compounding food crises.

### Caribbean

**Countries:** Jamaica, Haiti, Cuba, Belize, Honduras, Dominican Republic, Trinidad and Tobago, and smaller island states

- Caribbean reef fisheries support approximately **6 million people** directly.
- Reef tourism generates **$7.9 billion/year** across the Caribbean, a dominant sector of many island economies.
- Haiti and Jamaica have particularly high dependence -- **fishing is one of the few accessible livelihoods** for coastal poor.

> "In the Caribbean, coral reef-associated tourism and fisheries provide direct livelihood support for millions of people, and the degradation of these ecosystems threatens both food security and economic stability."
> -- Burke, L., et al. (2011). *Reefs at Risk Revisited*. World Resources Institute. (https://www.wri.org/research/reefs-risk-revisited)

**Specific vulnerability:** Caribbean reefs have already lost **>50% of live coral cover** since the 1970s. Communities face compounding risks from hurricanes, which are also intensifying.

### East Africa

**Countries:** Tanzania, Kenya, Mozambique, Madagascar, Comoros, Mauritius, Seychelles

- An estimated **10+ million people** along the East African coast depend on reef fisheries for food and income.
- In Tanzania and Mozambique, artisanal reef fishing is the **primary income** for most coastal villages.
- Madagascar's coastal communities are among the **most nutritionally dependent on reef fish** globally.

> "In East Africa, coral reef fisheries are essential for the food security and livelihoods of millions of coastal people, particularly in Tanzania, Mozambique, and Madagascar, where alternative protein sources are scarce."
> -- CORDIO East Africa (Coastal Oceans Research and Development - Indian Ocean). (https://cordioea.net/)

**Specific vulnerability:** These regions have **the least adaptive capacity** -- limited infrastructure, low GDP per capita, and few economic alternatives. Reef collapse would translate almost directly into hunger.

### Summary: Global Dependence

| Region | People Dependent | Primary Dependence | Alternatives Available |
|--------|------------------|--------------------|----------------------|
| Coral Triangle (SE Asia) | 120+ million | Fisheries, tourism | Very limited |
| Pacific Islands | 10+ million | Fisheries (50-90% of protein) | Minimal |
| Caribbean | 6+ million | Tourism, fisheries | Moderate (varies) |
| East Africa | 10+ million | Artisanal fisheries | Very limited |

## 7. What Policies Exist?

### Paris Agreement -- 1.5 degrees C Target

The 2015 Paris Agreement set a goal of limiting global warming to **1.5 degrees C above pre-industrial levels**, with 2 degrees C as an outer limit. For coral reefs, this distinction is existential:

- At **1.5 degrees C warming:** An estimated **70-90% of coral reefs** would be lost.
- At **2 degrees C warming:** Virtually **99%+ of coral reefs** would be lost.

> "Coral reefs would decline by 70-90 percent with global warming of 1.5 degrees C, whereas virtually all (>99 percent) would be lost with 2 degrees C."
> -- IPCC Special Report on Global Warming of 1.5 degrees C (2018), Chapter 3. (https://www.ipcc.ch/sr15/chapter/chapter-3/)

Current warming is approximately **1.2-1.3 degrees C** above pre-industrial levels and tracking toward **2.5-3 degrees C** by 2100 under current policies.

### Marine Protected Areas (MPAs)

- **30x30 target:** The Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (2022) set a target of **30% of ocean areas protected by 2030**.
- Currently, only about **8% of the ocean** is in designated MPAs, and a much smaller fraction is effectively managed.
- MPAs can protect reefs from overfishing and local disturbances but **cannot protect against warming ocean temperatures**.

> "Marine Protected Areas are an essential tool for reducing local stressors on coral reefs, but they cannot on their own address the primary threat of climate-driven warming."
> -- IUCN, "Marine Protected Areas and Climate Change." (https://www.iucn.org/resources/issues-brief/marine-protected-areas-and-climate-change)

### ICRI Framework

The **International Coral Reef Initiative (ICRI)** is a global partnership of governments, international organizations, and NGOs dedicated to coral reef conservation:

- Adopted the **"Recommendation on the Conservation of Coral Reefs and Related Ecosystems in the Face of Climate Change"** urging nations to take urgent action.
- Promotes **integrated coastal management**, sustainable fisheries, and reef restoration.
- Called for coral reefs to be central in **climate adaptation planning**.

> "ICRI calls on all governments to urgently reduce greenhouse gas emissions in line with the Paris Agreement, and to integrate coral reef conservation into national climate adaptation and biodiversity strategies."
> -- ICRI, "Plan of Action 2021-2024." (https://icriforum.org/documents/icri-plan-of-action-2021-2024/)

### Other Relevant Policies

- **Coral Reef Conservation Act (U.S.):** Established NOAA's Coral Reef Conservation Program.
- **EU Biodiversity Strategy 2030:** Includes commitments to protect marine ecosystems.
- **Regional fisheries management organizations (RFMOs):** Set catch limits for reef-associated fisheries.
- **Local watershed management:** Reducing land-based pollution runoff to improve reef resilience.

## 8. Common Misconceptions

### Misconception 1: "Deeper reefs are safe"

**Reality:** Mesophotic and deep reefs face many of the same threats as shallow reefs. Research shows deep reefs bleach comparably during mass events (see Section 5.2). They also have distinct species communities -- meaning they cannot simply "reseed" shallow reefs.

> "Deep reefs of the Great Barrier Reef offer limited thermal refuge during mass coral bleaching."
> -- Frade et al. (2018), *Nature Communications*. (https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-05741-0)

### Misconception 2: "It's just about temperature"

**Reality:** While temperature is the dominant driver, bleaching outcomes depend on multiple interacting factors: light intensity, nutrient levels, water flow, sediment load, disease presence, and the coral's own thermal history and genetic makeup (see Section 5.4).

A reef at 30 degrees C with high light, low flow, and elevated nutrients will bleach far more severely than a reef at 30 degrees C with moderate light, good circulation, and clean water.

### Misconception 3: "Bleaching = death"

**Reality:** Bleaching is a **stress response**, not necessarily a death sentence. Corals can recover from bleaching if:
- Thermal stress is removed within **approximately 4-8 weeks**.
- The coral has energy reserves to survive the period without zooxanthellae.
- No secondary infections (disease) take hold during the weakened state.

However, **severe or repeated bleaching** does lead to mass mortality. Recovery from severe bleaching can take **10-15 years under ideal conditions**, and many reefs are now bleaching faster than they can recover.

> "Bleached corals are not dead, but they are stressed and more susceptible to disease and mortality. If the stress is relieved in time, corals can recover. However, if bleaching is severe or prolonged, widespread mortality results."
> -- Great Barrier Reef Marine Park Authority. "Coral bleaching." (https://www.gbrmpa.gov.au/our-work/threats-to-the-reef/coral-bleaching)

### Misconception 4: "Coral reefs only matter for biodiversity"

**Reality:** Reefs provide direct economic value worth trillions of dollars, protect coastlines from storm damage, support food security for 500+ million people, and are a source of medical compounds. Their collapse is a **human development crisis**, not just an environmental one.

### Misconception 5: "Corals can just adapt"

**Reality:** While there is evidence of some thermal adaptation, the **rate of warming far exceeds the rate of natural adaptation**. Corals reproduce slowly (many species are long-lived), and genetic adaptation takes many generations.

> "The current rate of ocean warming is likely too fast for most coral species to adapt through natural evolutionary processes."
> -- Hughes, T.P., et al. (2017). "Global warming and recurrent mass bleaching of corals." *Nature*, 543, 373-377. (https://doi.org/10.1038/nature21707)

## 9. Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **DHW (Degree Heating Weeks)** | A cumulative measure of thermal stress. Calculated as the sum of SST anomalies (above the maximum monthly mean) over a 12-week rolling window, measured in degrees C-weeks. DHW >= 4 indicates bleaching likely; DHW >= 8 indicates severe bleaching and mortality likely. |
| **SST (Sea Surface Temperature)** | The temperature of the ocean surface layer (top ~1 meter). Measured by satellites (e.g., NOAA CRW) and in-situ sensors. The primary variable used to assess thermal stress on coral reefs. |
| **SSTA (Sea Surface Temperature Anomaly)** | The deviation of current SST from a long-term climatological mean (usually the 1985-2012 baseline for NOAA CRW). Positive SSTA indicates warmer-than-average conditions. |
| **Zooxanthellae** | Symbiotic dinoflagellate algae (genus *Symbiodinium*, now reclassified as family Symbiodiniaceae) that live within coral tissues. They photosynthesize and provide up to 90% of the coral's energy. Their expulsion under stress causes bleaching. |
| **Bleaching** | A stress response in which corals expel their zooxanthellae, turning white. Bleached corals are alive but starving and vulnerable. Caused primarily by thermal stress but also by light stress, freshwater exposure, pollution, and disease. |
| **Mortality** | The death of coral tissue. Distinguished from bleaching in that mortality is irreversible. Mass mortality follows severe or prolonged bleaching when corals cannot recover their zooxanthellae. |
| **Thermal Tolerance** | The range of temperatures a coral can withstand without bleaching. Varies by species, population, and acclimatization history. Populations in naturally variable thermal environments tend to have higher thermal tolerance. |
| **MPA (Marine Protected Area)** | A designated area of ocean where human activities (fishing, development, extraction) are restricted to protect ecosystems. Effective MPAs reduce local stressors but cannot protect against ocean warming. |
| **Coral Triangle** | The marine region bounded by Indonesia, Philippines, Malaysia, Papua New Guinea, Solomon Islands, and Timor-Leste. Contains 76% of all known coral species and is the global epicenter of marine biodiversity. |
| **Mesophotic Reef** | Coral reef ecosystems found at depths of 30-150 meters, in the "twilight zone" of light penetration. Once thought to be refugia for shallow reef species, but research shows they face similar threats. |
| **HotSpot** | In NOAA Coral Reef Watch's system, a HotSpot is the positive SST anomaly above the maximum monthly mean SST (MMM) climatology. HotSpot >= 1 degree C triggers a Bleaching Warning. |
| **MMM (Maximum Monthly Mean)** | The highest monthly mean SST value over a baseline period (typically 1985-2012). Used as the threshold above which thermal stress accumulates. |
| **El Nino** | A climate pattern characterized by anomalous warming of the central/eastern tropical Pacific Ocean. El Nino events intensify ocean warming globally and have triggered all four global coral bleaching events. |
| **PAR (Photosynthetically Active Radiation)** | The spectrum of light (400-700 nm) used by photosynthetic organisms. High PAR intensity amplifies bleaching by increasing oxidative stress in zooxanthellae under thermal stress. |
| **SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)** | A machine learning interpretability framework that assigns each feature a contribution value for a given prediction. Used in our analysis to identify which environmental and human factors most strongly predict bleaching risk. |
